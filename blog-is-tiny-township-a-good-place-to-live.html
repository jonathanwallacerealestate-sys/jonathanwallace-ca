Is Tiny Township a good place to live?

Georgian Bay market · Updated June 2026 · ~3 min read

Tiny Township is a good place to live if the beach is the point. The township runs along some of the clearest water on southern Georgian Bay, from Balm Beach through Lafontaine to the edge of Awenda Provincial Park, and daily life organizes itself around that shoreline. Here is the honest picture, the same one Jonathan Wallace gives buyers who ask, because he lives and works one township over in Midland.

What life in Tiny actually looks like

Tiny is not a town with a main street. It is a long stretch of shoreline and concession roads, with beaches numbered like old friends and small clusters of community around them. You drive to Midland or Penetanguishene for groceries, hospitals, and most services, usually 10 to 25 minutes depending on where in the township you are. People who love Tiny love exactly this: quiet roads, water at the end of the street, and neighbours who wave.

The four things to understand before you buy here

  1. Many homes started life as cottages. Some have been fully converted to year-round homes and some only look like they have. Insulation, foundations, heating systems, and winterized water lines vary house to house, and the difference matters in February.
  2. Wells and septics are the norm. Budget for inspections and know the age of both systems before you waive conditions.
  3. Shore road allowances and beach access are local knowledge. Which stretches of beach are public, which are contested, and what that means for a specific property is something to confirm per address, not assume.
  4. The rhythm is seasonal. Summer brings beach traffic and full neighbourhoods; winter is quiet, beautiful, and demands a plan for snow and heat. Most people find they love one season first and grow into the other.

Who Tiny suits, and who it does not

Tiny suits beach people, remote workers who want the water more than a commute, young families who want childhood summers to look like childhood summers, and retirees trading city noise for shoreline. It suits less well if you want walkable amenities, transit, or a home that asks nothing of you: rural properties come with maintenance, and the township's charm depends on being fine with driving for your groceries.

What it costs and what you get

Tiny's market runs from modest inland homes to significant waterfront, and the mix of seasonal and year-round properties means prices vary more per street than in a typical town. Rather than quote a number that will be stale by the time you read this, the practical move is to ask for current sold data for the specific area of the township you are considering. Ask Jonathan Wallace for it directly, or watch recent Tiny Township home tours to calibrate what your budget buys.

The verdict

If your ideal Saturday ends with sand in the car, Tiny Township is one of the best places to live in Ontario, and the people who move here rarely move back to town. Go in understanding wells, septics, winterization, and the seasonal rhythm, and it will give you exactly the life the beach photos promise.
