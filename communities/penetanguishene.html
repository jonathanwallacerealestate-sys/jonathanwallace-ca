Penetanguishene Real Estate: A Local's Guide to Living and Buying Here

A charming bilingual harbour town with deep history, scenic waterfront and a tight-knit community feel.

Price my Penetanguishene homeSee listings

By Jonathan Wallace, REALTOR®, Salesperson, Faris Team Real Estate, Brokerage

Penetanguishene sits just across a small valley from Midland, a bilingual harbour town with the better public waterfront of the two and a shoreline that holds more homes on the water. In daily life the two towns are almost one, and residents use each other constantly.

What's it like to live in Penetanguishene?

Penetanguishene is a bilingual harbour town of about 10,000 people that shares nearly everything with neighbouring Midland while keeping its own quieter, waterfront-first character.

Main Street runs down to the harbour, Rotary Champlain Wendat Park puts a beach, splash pad, and trails right on the water, and Discovery Harbour preserves the town's naval history. Penetanguishene has grown faster than Midland in recent years, and residents cross the valley to Midland for the hospital and big-box shopping without thinking twice. You are not choosing between two regions here, you are choosing which end of one community you sleep in.

The town leans into its waterfront and its history. Discovery Harbour recreates the early naval and military past on the shore, the town dock is a departure point for Georgian Bay island cruises, and the bilingual community keeps a full calendar of events through the year. For a town of its size, there is a lot happening within walking distance of the water.

Penetanguishene is where I bought my very first house, so I know it from the ground up. A big part of its character is the Francophone community, which gives the town a different feel from Midland even though little more than a valley and a golf course separates the two.

Waterfront vs. in-town living in Penetanguishene

Penetanguishene has proportionally more waterfront homes than Midland because the town stretches further along the Georgian Bay shoreline, and that shoreline is a mix of original cottages and newer year-round builds.

That variety is the draw: you can find an older cottage to reimagine or a modern waterfront home, alongside in-town streets near Main Street that are walkable and more affordable. The public waterfront at Rotary Park is genuinely better used than Midland's harbour, which is part of why buyers who want the water at the centre of their week gravitate here.

The waterfront sits in a few distinct pockets: Beck Boulevard, along Champlain Road, and up off Sandy Bay, with more shoreline homes on Gordon and Gilwood. Because much of it faces west with generous frontage, prices span a wide range, from around $1 million to estates near $4.85 million out on Sandy Bay. For deep-water boating, Penetanguishene also has world-class marinas, Hindson among them.

Is Penetanguishene good for year-round living or mostly seasonal?

Penetanguishene is a four-season town with year-round employment and full services, anchored by two of the area's largest employers, the Central North Correctional Centre and the Waypoint Centre for Mental Health Care.

Add the manufacturing employers up off Fuller Avenue and you have a town that works twelve months a year, not a summer strip. It is on full municipal services in the core, has its own schools and arenas, and leans on Midland only for the hospital and the largest stores. For year-round buyers that mix of waterfront and stability is the appeal.

What do homes in Penetanguishene cost right now?

Penetanguishene tracks closely with Midland on the everyday side of the market, and its average runs slightly higher, mostly because a larger share of its inventory is newer homes. The housing ranges widely, from homes dating to the early 1900s on stone foundations up to one-acre estate lots with million-dollar properties. Waterfront is its own market, running from about $1 million to multi-million-dollar estates.

One local note worth budgeting for: property taxes on a similar home tend to run slightly higher in Penetanguishene than in Midland, because a similar municipal load is spread across fewer residents. It is rarely a dealbreaker, but it belongs in your monthly math. As always, confirm current prices and the exact tax figure for the specific home.

The housing stock itself is varied: modest in-town bungalows, established family homes, original waterfront cottages, and a growing number of newer builds on the shoreline. That range is part of why Penetanguishene appeals to such a wide set of buyers, from first-timers to people downsizing onto the water. Whatever the type, ask for recent comparable sales in that specific pocket, because waterfront and in-town values move on different clocks.

Commuting from Penetanguishene: Barrie, the GTA, and beyond

Penetanguishene is about 45 minutes from Barrie, a few minutes further than Midland, and roughly two hours from the Toronto area, which makes a hybrid commute practical and a daily one long.

The drive south runs through Midland and down Highway 93 to the 400 corridor at Barrie. Most buyers who work in the city do so remotely for part of the week and treat the commute as an occasional trip rather than a daily grind.

Schools, language and amenities in Penetanguishene

Penetanguishene has a strong Francophone community and French-language schooling, but there is currently no English-language high school in town, so English-stream high schoolers are bused into Midland.

On the other side of the ledger, Penetanguishene's Burkevale Protestant Separate School is one of the area's most sought-after schools, popular enough that families drive their children in from other communities for it. The town has its own arenas and library and shares the hospital and YMCA with Midland. If you plan to build, one practical advantage buyers mention often is that Penetanguishene's building department tends to be more straightforward to work with than its neighbours.

Penetanguishene-specific things buyers should know

If you plan to build or renovate, Penetanguishene is generally more progressive to work with than the surrounding municipalities, but any waterfront purchase still needs the shore road allowance, septic, and water-level checks that come with Georgian Bay shoreline.

Because the town has more original cottages and waterfront stock than Midland, you are more likely to be buying a property on a well and septic system or one that started life as a seasonal cottage, so winterization and servicing checks matter. Confirm the school busing situation if you have teenagers, and factor the slightly higher tax rate into your numbers.

Frequently asked questions about Penetanguishene real estate

Is Penetanguishene a French town?

Penetanguishene is officially bilingual and has a strong Francophone community with French-language schooling, alongside a large English-speaking population. It is one of the more distinctly bilingual towns in the region.

What is the difference between Midland and Penetanguishene?

Midland is the larger service hub with the hospital, big-box stores, and the area's main high schools, while Penetanguishene is a smaller bilingual harbour town with better public waterfront, more homes on the water, and an easier building department. They sit minutes apart and residents use both daily. There is a full comparison in the Midland vs. Penetanguishene guide on this site.

Are there beaches in Penetanguishene?

Yes. Rotary Champlain Wendat Park offers a public beach, splash pad, and waterfront trails right in town, which is one of the reasons buyers who want walkable water access favour Penetanguishene.

Does Penetanguishene have a high school?

Penetanguishene has French-language schooling but currently no English-language high school, so English-stream high schoolers are bused to Midland. The town's Burkevale Protestant Separate School is well regarded and draws families from outside the community.

Is Penetanguishene good for waterfront property?

Penetanguishene has proportionally more waterfront homes than Midland because it stretches further along the Georgian Bay shoreline, with a mix of original cottages and newer year-round builds. It is a strong choice for buyers who want the water at the centre of daily life.
